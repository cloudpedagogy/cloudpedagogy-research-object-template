\chapter{Overview}\label{overview}

A multivariable logistic regression model trained on a synthetic
snapshot of 10,000 outpatient records to identify factors associated
with missed appointments.

\chapter{Intended Use}\label{intended-use}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Primary Use Cases}: Demonstration of analytical workflow
  within the Quarto Healthcare Research Object template.
\item
  \textbf{Out-of-Scope Uses}: Any clinical, operational, scheduling, or
  real-world predictive application. The model predicts synthetic noise
  and manufactured relationships.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Metrics \& Performance}\label{metrics-performance}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Primary Metrics}: Odds Ratios (OR) with 95\% Confidence
  Intervals.
\item
  \textbf{Performance}: Given the synthetic nature, standard
  discriminatory metrics (like AUC) are artificially high
  (\textasciitilde0.75) due to the programmed signal-to-noise ratio in
  the generation script.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Training Data}\label{training-data}

The model was fit on the entirety of the
\texttt{synthetic\_healthcare\_example.csv} dataset. See the Dataset
Card for details.

\chapter{Ethical Considerations}\label{ethical-considerations}

While the model is synthetic, similar real-world models often risk
penalizing patients living in high deprivation areas if used punitively
(e.g., overbooking patients predicted to miss appointments).
Interventions based on such models must focus on patient support (e.g.,
transport assistance) rather than penalization.
